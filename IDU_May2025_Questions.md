## Section A — Factual & Comprehension Questions

### Q1 (Sciences — Ref 1)
Identify **two** health monitoring devices shown in Reference 1 and state what each device measures. Use appropriate scientific units in your answer.

> *Targets: smart glucose monitor (blood glucose concentration — mmol/L), smart heart monitor (blood pressure — mmHg), smart watch, smart scale*

### Q2 (Sciences — Ref 1)
Explain how a **smart glucose monitor** collects and transmits data to a mobile phone app. Include at least one scientific concept in your answer.

### Q3 (Language & Literature — Ref 2)
According to the video "Where does my data go?" (Reference 2), identify **three** rights that companies gain when users agree to their terms and conditions.

### Q4 (Language & Literature — Ref 2)
The video states: *"The desire to access the app is greater than the desire to protect the personal data."* Explain what this statement means in your own words.

### Q5 (Sciences — Ref 1)
A smart heart monitor displays a blood pressure reading of 130/85 mmHg. Explain what the two numbers in a blood pressure reading represent and whether this reading would be considered healthy. Use scientific terminology in your answer.

## Section B — Conceptual & Analytical Questions

### Q6 (Language & Literature — Ref 5)
The advertising campaign (Reference 5) uses the phrase *"One small step on the scale, one giant step for your health."*

(a) Identify the **original quotation** this slogan is based on and explain its significance.

(b) Explain **why** the advertisers chose to adapt this famous quotation. What effect does this **intertextual reference** have on the audience?

### Q7 (Language & Literature — Ref 4)
Using Reference 4 (Journalistic text types), explain the difference between a **news report** and an **opinion editorial**. Why is it important for readers to distinguish between these text types when reading about health technology?

### Q8 (Sciences — Ref 2 & 3)
Using evidence from References 2 and 3, explain how personal health data collected by mobile apps could be used by corporations for purposes **other than** health monitoring. Discuss at least **two** possible uses.

### Q9 (Interdisciplinary — Ref 2)
The video (Reference 2) uses several **rhetorical questions** (e.g., *"How can the user of the mobile app protect their personal data?"* and *"Do you have any control over your privacy?"*).

(a) From a **language and literature** perspective, explain the persuasive effect of rhetorical questions in this context.

(b) From a **science** perspective, suggest **two** technological solutions that could help users protect their personal health data.

### Q10 (Language & Literature — Ref 5)
Analyse the **poster advert** in Reference 5. Comment on how the following elements work together to persuade the audience:
- Visual imagery
- Colour scheme
- Text and font choices
- Use of slogan

## Section C — Interdisciplinary Synthesis Questions

### Q11 (Interdisciplinary — Ref 1, 2 & 3)
The Statement of Inquiry states: *"Understanding the purpose and function of development in technology should include understanding the opportunities and risks."*

Using evidence from **at least two references**, discuss the opportunities and risks of using smart health monitoring devices. Structure your answer to include both **scientific** and **language and literature** perspectives.

### Q12 (Interdisciplinary — Ref 3)
Reference 3 presents four different perspectives on data collection.

(a) Summarise **two** of these perspectives.

(b) Explain how these perspectives demonstrate that the issue of data collection involves **both scientific understanding and ethical/social communication**.

### Q13 (Interdisciplinary — Ref 4 & 5)
A new health-monitoring device has just been launched. You have been asked to write a **journalistic piece** (using the text types from Reference 4) promoting public awareness of **both** the benefits and the data privacy concerns of this device.

(a) Choose **one** journalistic text type from Reference 4 and explain why it would be the most appropriate for this task.

(b) Write the **opening paragraph** of this piece (approximately 80–120 words).

### Q14 (Interdisciplinary — all references)
To what extent does the advertising campaign (Reference 5) accurately represent the scientific reality of how health monitoring devices work? Use evidence from References 1 and 2 to support your argument.

### Q15 (Interdisciplinary — Ref 2 & 5)
Compare and contrast the **purpose and audience** of the video in Reference 2 ("Where does my data go?") with the advertising video in Reference 5. Consider:
- Tone and language
- Intended message
- Use of persuasive techniques
- Target audience

## Section D — Evaluative & Debatable Questions

### Q16 (Debatable — Global Context)
*"The benefits of health monitoring technology outweigh the risks to personal privacy."*

To what extent do you agree with this statement? Use evidence from the pre-release material **and** your knowledge from MYP subject groups to support your argument.

### Q17 (Debatable — Ref 2)
The video (Reference 2) states that *"privacy policies were originally meant to protect users, but now they give corporations more rights to your data than you might realize."*

Evaluate the extent to which **governments** should regulate how technology companies collect and use personal health data. Consider both scientific and societal perspectives.

### Q18 (Evaluative — Ref 5)
Evaluate the **effectiveness** of the advertising campaign in Reference 5 in motivating people to use health monitoring technology. Consider:
- The persuasive techniques used in the video jingle
- The design of the poster advert
- Whether the campaign addresses the concerns raised in References 2 and 3

### Q19 (Debatable — Global Context)
Some people argue that advances in health monitoring technology represent genuine scientific progress, while others argue that they primarily serve corporate profit motives.

Using your understanding of the global context **Scientific and Technical Innovation** and evidence from the pre-release material, discuss **both sides** of this argument and reach a justified conclusion.

## Section E — Skills-Based & Applied Questions

### Q20 (Sciences — Applied)
A new health monitoring app claims to track a user's heart rate, blood glucose, sleep patterns, and physical activity. Design a **simple data table** showing what data each sensor would collect, the units of measurement, and how frequently the data should be recorded. Justify your choices scientifically.

### Q21 (Language & Literature — Applied)
Write a **privacy policy summary** (approximately 100–150 words) for a fictional health monitoring app that balances the company's need for data with the user's right to privacy. Use appropriate formal language and refer to the concerns raised in Reference 2.

### Q22 (Interdisciplinary — Applied)
You have been asked to create an **awareness campaign** for teenagers about the risks of sharing personal health data through mobile apps.

(a) Describe **two** techniques from the advertising campaign (Reference 5) that you could adapt for your awareness campaign.

(b) Explain how you would use **scientific evidence** to make your campaign credible.

(c) Explain how you would use **language and literary techniques** to make your campaign persuasive.

- Suggest why there may be variations in the readings

### Q24 (Language & Literature — Textual Analysis)
*[This question would likely include a new/unseen text in the exam — e.g., a news article or advertisement about health tech.]*

Analyse the language, tone, and structure of the text provided. How does the author use language to position the reader to accept or question the use of health monitoring technology?

Explain how the **interdisciplinary** study of both **Language & Literature** and **Sciences** helps us develop a more complete understanding of the impact of health monitoring technology on society. Use specific examples from the pre-release material to support your answer.

## Key Preparation Tips

> [!TIP]
> **For the exam, be ready to:**
> - Link the **Statement of Inquiry** to specific references
> - Use **scientific terminology** (mmol/L, mmHg, data, variables, accuracy, reliability)
> - Identify **language techniques** (rhetorical questions, intertextuality, persuasive devices, tone)
> - Compare **different perspectives** (Reference 3) and evaluate bias
> - Distinguish **fact from opinion** using journalistic text types (Reference 4)
> - Analyse **visual and textual advertising techniques** (Reference 5)
> - Connect everything back to the global context: **Scientific and Technical Innovation**

> The exam WILL contain **new, unfamiliar material** alongside the pre-release sources. Practice synthesizing new information with what you already know from the references.
